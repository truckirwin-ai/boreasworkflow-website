**Jeremy Sharp owns a direct competitor.** ReverbReports (reverbreports.com, "Reverb") is an AI report-generation platform for testing psychologists, founded by Dr. Jeremy Sharp and "founding partners." It is the closest product to our clinical track on the entire page. Sharp is simultaneously the podcast host, the consulting service, and a competing founder.

This changes the outreach math (see the last section). It does not make Sharp useless to us; it makes the "pitch him a neutral interview and sponsor the show" plan naive.

## What this tells us about the competitive landscape

1. **The niche's category leader is cloud, and it is Reverb.** The most direct competitor to our clinical track is web-based AI report generation where PHI leaves the clinician's machine to a vendor server. That is exactly the architecture our entire wedge argues against. The category leader being cloud is a gift, not a threat: it gives our local-first, UNID-redaction, no-cloud-PHI story a concrete foil in the one niche we most want to win.

2. **Nobody on this page is forensic.** Every tool here is clinical / assessment / practice-ops. Reverb, NovoPsych, Pearson all aim at the testing psychologist doing clinical evals. None address cross-examination, audit trail for discovery, or court-facing defensibility. Our forensic track has no competitor on this list at all. That confirms the plan's read: the forensic track is open water; the fight is on the clinical track.

3. **The stack is fragmented and cloud-default.** A working testing practice here is stitching Reverb (reports) + NovoPsych or Pearson (administration/scoring) + TherapyNotes/IntakeQ (EHR/intake) + PracticeVital (BI). All cloud, all sending PHI somewhere. Our local-first single-workspace pitch is a consolidation and a privacy argument at once.

4. **Our compare page already handles all of them without naming them.** compare.html buckets the world into "Cloud AI drafters," "Practice-management + AI add-ons," and Boreas. Reverb and NovoPsych are the faces of bucket one; TherapyNotes and IntakeQ are bucket two. We do not need to name Reverb on the site; we need the category contrast to be sharp. It is.

## How Boreas differentiates against Reverb specifically

Reverb is the competitor to beat on the clinical track. The contrast, feature by feature:

- **PHI location:** Reverb is a cloud web portal; case data goes to their servers. Boreas is local-first; case data never leaves the clinician's machine, and anything bound for an LLM is UNID-redacted first. This is the whole argument.
- **Diagnostic authority:** Reverb is a report drafter. Boreas gates drafting behind a signed diagnostic decision; the doctor diagnoses first, always, and the writing assistant only drafts after. "Doctor always diagnoses" is a structural safeguard, not a tagline.
- **Defensibility:** Reverb has no forensic/audit story. Boreas audit-logs every diagnostic decision and exports the decision trail for discovery. On the clinical track this reads as payer-audit readiness; on forensic it is cross-examination survival.
- **Scope:** Reverb is clinical/testing only. Boreas serves clinical and forensic from one product.
- **Pricing shape:** Reverb meters reports (Essential up to 5/mo). Boreas is seat-based (Pro $299/mo, founder $199 for life), no per-report throttle. For a high-volume testing practice, per-report metering is a ceiling; seat pricing is not.

Where Reverb may be ahead today: a mature web UX, an established user base, and the founder's direct credibility with the exact audience. We should not pretend those away.

## Implication for GTM: the Sharp outreach must change

The queued morning-brief action "send Jeremy Sharp an interview + sponsorship email" was written before we knew Sharp owns Reverb. Reassess before sending:

- **Do not hand a competitor a product briefing.** The current draft explains what Boreas does and how it differs on exactly the axis where Reverb is weak (cloud PHI). Sending that to Reverb's founder is free competitive intel for them, with little upside for us.
- **Sponsorship still technically works but is awkward.** Paying to advertise on the show of a competitor's founder, who will not give a competing product a neutral guest spot, is weak leverage for our $800/mo podcast line. His CE runs through At Health; his audience is capturable other ways (the Facebook community, other assessment CE instructors, direct outbound to the same licensees).
- **Recommended reframe:** treat Sharp as a competitor to study, not a channel to buy. Keep monitoring Reverb (pricing, feature releases, positioning). Redirect the podcast-sponsorship budget and the "borrowed trust" play toward voices who are not competitors: assessment CE instructors, practice consultants for testing psychologists, and the Testing Psychologist Facebook community itself (soft participation, not a Sharp deal).
- **If Robert still wants contact with Sharp,** it should be founder-to-founder and honest ("I built a local-first, forensic-capable tool; I know you built Reverb; is there a non-competing collaboration here"), not a disguised sponsorship pitch. Lower priority than the non-competing channels.
